# Hospice Volunteer Retention: International Best Practices

> A synthesis of actionable research findings from the **UK**, **Canada**, and **Australia** on improving hospice volunteer retention. Sources are cited at the end of each section.

---

## 1. United Kingdom 🇬🇧

### Key Findings

- **Retention is significantly more cost-effective than recruitment.** Experienced volunteers require less supervision, deliver higher-quality service, mentor newcomers, and strengthen community culture. *(Cape Consulting, 2024)*
- **Volunteers don't leave from lack of passion — they leave from structural breakdowns.** The top reasons for departure are: unclear expectations, sudden policy changes, feeling unheard, poor communication, infrequent feedback, feeling disconnected from impact, and overcommitment alongside work/family responsibilities. *(Cape Consulting; NCVO Time Well Spent 2023)*
- **Differentiated strategies are essential.** Generic retention approaches don't work for all volunteer groups. Younger volunteers are influenced by transitions into paid employment; older volunteers are transitioning out of employment. *(St. Andrews research, Hopkins & Dowell, 2021)*

### Actionable Recommendations

| # | Recommendation | Detail |
|---|----------------|--------|
| 1 | **Structured onboarding with role clarity** | Define expectations, scope, and boundaries before the first shift. Avoid assigning tasks far removed from the agreed role without discussion. |
| 2 | **Regular capacity check-ins** | Ask: *"What do I realistically have capacity for right now?"* and *"What would help me recover this week?"* — normalizes sustainable contribution over "commitment at all costs." |
| 3 | **70-20-10 development model** | Reframe training as growth: 70% learning-through-doing, 20% learning-through-others (peer mentoring), 10% formal training courses. Volunteers stay when they feel they're growing, not just filling a gap. |
| 4 | **Connect tasks to impact** | Volunteers stay when they see their contribution makes a difference. Regularly share patient/family feedback and organisational outcomes. |
| 5 | **Segment communications** | Tailor messaging by volunteer demographics, motivations, and role type — avoid one-size-fits-all updates. |
| 6 | **Track retention metrics** | Monitor retention rate, no-show rate, satisfaction data, and training completion rates to identify patterns early rather than reacting after departure. |

**Sources:** Cape Consulting UK (volunteer retention strategies); NCVO *Time Well Spent 2023*; Hopkins & Dowell (2021), St. Andrews; SAGE Journals — Predicting Attrition Among Hospice Volunteers.

## 2. Canada 🇨🇦

### Key Findings

- **Comprehensive training + structured peer support = higher retention, lower burnout.** Research hypothesis confirmed: organisations with robust training programs and formal peer-support systems show statistically significant improvements in volunteer retention. *(Canadian Hospice Volunteer Support & Retention Study; Claxton-Oldfield & Jones, 2013)*
- **Altruism is the primary motivator, but emotional distress from grief exposure drives turnover.** Volunteers are drawn by compassion but face significant emotional burden from repeated exposure to loss and dying. *(ThesisLaunch — Canadian Hospice Volunteer Experiences)*
- **The volunteer coordinator role is pivotal.** Coordinators who provide ongoing emotional support, clear role boundaries, and timely debriefing significantly reduce volunteer attrition. *(HPCO; Pesut et al., 2012)*
- **Careful pre-screening improves retention.** Screening for emotional readiness, motivations, and suitability before entry into the hospice volunteer role predicts longer tenure. *(SAGE Journals — Predicting Attrition, 2019)*

### Actionable Recommendations

| # | Recommendation | Detail |
|---|----------------|--------|
| 1 | **Implement structured peer-support groups** | Create regular (monthly) facilitated sessions where volunteers can process grief, share experiences, and build mutual support — not optional, but embedded in the program. |
| 2 | **Enhance pre-screening for emotional readiness** | Assess candidates' motivations, coping strategies, and emotional resilience during intake — not just availability and interest. |
| 3 | **Invest in the coordinator role** | Ensure volunteer coordinators have time, training, and organisational support to provide individualised debriefing and relationship-building. |
| 4 | **Match roles to skills and interests** | Carefully align volunteer assignments with personal strengths and preferences — mismatched roles accelerate burnout. |
| 5 | **Provide grief-specific training** | Don't rely on general volunteer training; include modules on anticipatory grief, loss, self-care, and boundary-setting specific to end-of-life contexts. |
| 6 | **Build patient-family relationship skills** | Train volunteers in the nuances of building therapeutic relationships with dying persons and their families — this is a distinct competency. |

**Sources:** Claxton-Oldfield & Jones (2013), *Holding on to What You Have Got*; Pesut et al. (2012), *Promoting Volunteer Capacity in Hospice Palliative Care*; HPCO — Hospice Volunteers: Roles and Retention; ThesisLaunch — Canadian Hospice Volunteer Support & Retention Study; SAGE Journals — Predicting Attrition Among Hospice Volunteers (2019).

## 3. Australia 🇦🇺

### Key Findings

- **Retention is the top concern among volunteer managers.** In the Queensland State of Volunteering report, retention ranked #1 among all volunteer-related sector issues. *(Volunteering Queensland, 2021)*
- **Retention isn't a discrete process — it's the cumulative result of overall volunteer management practice.** Organisations that treat retention as a standalone initiative fail; those that embed retention thinking into recruitment, onboarding, supervision, and recognition succeed. *(Volunteering Victoria; Volunteering Gateway)*
- **National Standards for Volunteer Involvement provide a proven framework.** The eight Standards (leadership & governance, recruitment, onboarding, support & development, safety & wellbeing, recognition, policies & practices) are the best-practice benchmark. *(Volunteering Australia; Volunteering Victoria)*
- **The "great volunteer resignation" is real and evidence-based.** Australia's Volunteering Research Papers initiative documented a systemic decline and recommends evidence-based actions with the greatest impact on minimising turnover. *(Volunteering Australia VRP, 2022)*

### Actionable Recommendations

| # | Recommendation | Detail |
|---|----------------|--------|
| 1 | **Adopt the National Standards for Volunteer Involvement as your framework** | Use the 8 Standards as a checklist to audit your current practices — gaps in standards alignment predict retention problems. |
| 2 | **Embed retention thinking into every stage** | Don't silo retention. Design recruitment messaging, onboarding content, supervision frequency, and recognition rituals all with retention as a design goal. |
| 3 | **Prioritise support & supervision** | Regular, scheduled supervision conversations (not just ad-hoc) give volunteers a reliable space to raise concerns before they escalate into departure decisions. |
| 4 | **Implement formal recognition & feedback loops** | Recognition doesn't need to be expensive — consistent, specific, timely feedback on contribution is more impactful than annual awards ceremonies. |
| 5 | **Manage performance concerns early** | Address role-fit issues, attendance patterns, and interpersonal tensions proactively; avoiding difficult conversations accelerates disengagement. |
| 6 | **Invest in training & development pathways** | Offer growth opportunities (leadership roles, specialist skills, mentorship positions) — volunteers who see a path forward stay longer. |

**Sources:** Volunteering Australia — *The Great (Volunteer) Resignation* (VRP, 2022); Volunteering Victoria — *Managing Volunteers for Retention*; Volunteering Gateway — retention strategies resource; NSW Government — *Recruitment and Retention of Volunteers: A Literature Review*; Volunteering Queensland — *State of Volunteering in Queensland 2021*.

## Cross-Country Synthesis: Universal Principles

Across all three countries, six universal principles emerge:

1. **Relationship-first management** — Volunteers are individuals with motivations, goals, and personal commitments, not interchangeable resources.
2. **Structured onboarding + role clarity** — The single most cited intervention across all three contexts.
3. **Ongoing emotional support** — Especially critical in hospice settings where grief exposure is continuous and cumulative.
4. **Growth pathways** — Volunteers stay when they see development, not just repetition.
5. **Impact visibility** — Connecting day-to-day tasks to patient/family outcomes sustains motivation.
6. **Sustainable expectations** — Organisations that support healthy boundaries and realistic commitments retain more volunteers than those that demand "commitment at all costs."
